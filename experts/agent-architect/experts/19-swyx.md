## 1. Profile and Background

### From Singapore to quantitative finance

Shawn Wang, universally known by the pseudonym **swyx**, grew up in Singapore and has had an atypical professional career that took him from Wall Street to the heart of the AI ecosystem. His first career took place entirely in the world of quantitative finance: he worked as a **derivatives trader** (currency options) and as a **TMT hedge fund analyst** (Technology, Media, Telecom), accumulating experience in leading institutions including **Two Sigma**, one of the most prestigious quantitative hedge funds in the world.

In finance, Wang earned approximately **$350,000 a year** -- a compensation he consciously chose to abandon to reinvent himself as a software developer. This radical decision, documented in an interview with freeCodeCamp, represents one of the most discussed career pivots in the tech community. He used free resources like freeCodeCamp to learn to program, demonstrating firsthand the philosophy he would later theorize.

### The technological transition and Developer Experience

After the move to tech, Wang built a career focused on **Developer Experience (DX)** at some of the most influential companies in the developer tools ecosystem:

- **Netlify** -- where he worked on the developer experience of the JAMstack platform, helping make Netlify one of the most beloved tools for frontend developers
- **Amazon Web Services (AWS)** -- where he led developer relations initiatives, particularly around AWS Amplify, working on technical communication and adoption of cloud tools
- **Temporal** -- the workflow orchestration platform, where he brought his DX expertise to a product oriented toward the reliability of distributed systems
- **Airbyte** -- the open-source platform for data integration, completing a path that spans frontend, cloud, orchestration and data engineering

All three of the last companies (Netlify, Temporal, Airbyte) are classified as **devtools unicorns** -- startups with valuation greater than one billion dollars -- which positions Wang as one of the developer relations professionals with the most consistent track record in the industry.

### The "Learn in Public" philosophy

Before becoming a central voice in the AI world, swyx was already widely recognized for having coined and promoted the concept of **"Learn in Public"** -- a learning philosophy that encourages developers to publicly share their growth path, instead of studying in isolation. The central idea is simple but powerful: write blog posts, make tutorials, record videos, answer questions on forums -- transform everything you learn into a public artifact. This approach, according to Wang, is the fastest way to build skills, reputation and professional network simultaneously.

Wang codified this philosophy in **"The Coding Career Handbook"**, a practical guide to a career in software development that has become a reference in the community. He has also moderated **r/reactjs** (over 200,000 developers), built the **Svelte Society** (from zero to over 15,000 members), and obtained recognitions such as GitHub Star and Stripe Community Expert.

This ability to build communities, combined with an analytical background coming from quantitative finance, created the unique profile that then made swyx one of the most influential voices in AI engineering.

## 2. Latent Space

### Origins and structure of the project

**Latent Space** is a multimedia publication launched in 2023 that includes a **Substack newsletter** and a **podcast** distributed on all major platforms (Apple Podcasts, Spotify, YouTube). The official subtitle is "The AI Engineer Podcast -- by and for AI Engineers", and the publication defines itself as the place to discover "how leading labs build Agents, Models, Infrastructure and AI for Science".

The project is co-hosted by **swyx** and **Alessio Fanelli**, partner at Decibel Partners, a venture capital fund focused on early-stage technical teams in infrastructure and applied AI. The combination of a builder/community leader (swyx) and an investor with technical vision (Fanelli) gives Latent Space a unique perspective that balances technical depth and understanding of market dynamics.

### Numbers and impact

In 2025, Latent Space has reached impressive numbers: **over 10 million readers and listeners**, with more than **175,000 newsletter subscribers** and thousands of paid subscribers. These data make it one of the most followed media in the entire AI ecosystem, surpassing in reach many traditional publications in the sector.

### Format and content

Latent Space operates with multiple content streams:

- **Main interviews**: in-depth conversations with the founders, researchers and builders behind the main AI projects, recorded predominantly in person at the San Francisco studio. The in-person format is a deliberate choice -- as swyx notes, "in-studio conversations are much more fluid and you can easily read the body language of someone who wants to chime in".

- **Monthly recap episodes**: "Alessio & Swyx" episodes that provide an audio version of the monthly recap written by swyx, offering a structured overview of the most important developments of the month.

- **AI News Roundup**: weekly updates on the most relevant news, produced in part with the help of personalized AI research agents (via Wang's smol AI project).

- **Science section**: content dedicated to AI for scientific discovery, with focus on materials, biology and other domains.

### Key guests and interviews

Latent Space has hosted exclusive interviews with leading figures from every corner of the AI ecosystem:

- **OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind (Gemini)** -- interviews with researchers and leaders of the main labs
- **Soumith Chintala** (Meta/PyTorch) -- on the future of open source AI
- **Bret Taylor** (Sierra AI) -- on enterprise application of AI
- **George Hotz** (Tiny Corp) -- on hardware and computational independence
- **Jon Frankle** (Databricks/MosaicML) -- on training and infrastructure
- **Chris Lattner** (Modular) -- on programming languages for AI (Mojo)
- **Jeremy Howard** (Answer.ai) -- on AI accessibility
- **Harrison Chase** (LangChain/LangGraph) -- on agent frameworks
- **Shunyu Yao** (Princeton) -- on ReAct, Tree of Thought, and CoALA

### Why Latent Space is influential

Latent Space's influence in the AI ecosystem derives from several converging factors. First, the **technical depth**: the interviews don't stop at the surface but go into the detail of architectures, design decisions and engineering trade-offs. Second, the **timing**: Latent Space has repeatedly anticipated trends that would become mainstream months later, from the centrality of agents to the Model Context Protocol. Third, the **credibility**: both swyx and Fanelli are active operators in the field, not external commentators, which gives their analyses a practical weight that is missing in many other publications. Finally, the **community**: around Latent Space an ecosystem of AI engineers, founders and investors has formed who use the podcast as a shared reference point for the language and conceptual frameworks of the sector.

## 3. "The AI Engineer"

### The founding essay: "The Rise of the AI Engineer"

In June 2023, swyx published on Latent Space what has become probably the most influential essay of the year in the applied AI ecosystem: **"The Rise of the AI Engineer"**. The central thesis is that a new engineering discipline was emerging, distinct from both traditional software engineering and machine learning engineering, specialized in the **productization of foundation models** and the construction of practical applications on top of them.

### The three definitions of the AI Engineer

Wang proposed a three-level taxonomy that has proven extraordinarily durable -- as he himself noted, he did not have to modify it in over two years:

1. **AI-augmented software engineer**: a traditional developer who uses AI coding tools (Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code) to increase their productivity. This is the most widespread and accessible form of AI engineering.

2. **Software engineer who builds AI products**: a developer who integrates foundation models into applications, working with APIs, prompt engineering, RAG, vector databases and orchestration. This is the "classic" definition of AI Engineer.

3. **Non-human software engineer**: a fully autonomous AI agent that writes code, like the coding agents (Devin, Claude Code, etc.). This is the emerging frontier that blurs the boundaries between tool and colleague.

### The fundamental distinction: AI Engineer vs ML Engineer

The essay articulates a critical distinction between two professional figures:

**ML Engineer:**
- Deals with the development of foundation models
- Requires skills in deep learning, PyTorch, large-scale data pipelines
- Must read and understand academic papers like "Attention Is All You Need"
- Extremely limited global population: about **5,000 researchers**
- Focus on pretraining, benchmarks, neural architectures

**AI Engineer:**
- Builds products on top of existing APIs and open-source models
- Requires software engineering skills, not deep learning
- Does not need to know PyTorch, read academic papers, or manage training infrastructure
- Works with prompt engineering, retrieval systems, vector databases, autonomous agents
- Potential population: a fraction of the **50 million global software developers**

### The five catalysts

Wang identifies five factors that make the emergence of the AI Engineer possible at this historical moment:

1. **The emergent capabilities of foundation models** exceed the original intent of the creators, allowing non-researchers to discover innovative applications that even the training teams had not foreseen.

2. **GPU scarcity** makes training economically prohibitive for most organizations; APIs become the dominant distribution mechanism.

3. **The speed advantage in development**: the "fire, ready, aim" workflow with LLMs allows for **1,000-10,000 times cheaper** validation compared to the traditional ML approach.

4. **The expansion of the talent pool**: JavaScript and Python developers can now participate in AI product development without the need for ML specialization.

5. **A different problem domain**: generative AI focuses on writing assistants, creative tools and conversational applications, rather than on fraud detection or recommendation systems.

### Predictions on the job market

Wang proposed specific and verifiable predictions: at the time of writing, there were approximately **10 times more ML Engineer positions** than AI Engineer on Indeed. His prediction is that this ratio will reverse within five years, making AI Engineering "the engineering job with the highest demand of the decade". Salary data supports this trend: AI Engineers at Anthropic earned about **$300,000** annually for prompt engineering work, while some positions at OpenAI reached **$900,000** for software-focused roles.

## 5. Conceptual Frameworks

### 5.1 Software 3.0: The evolution of the software paradigm

One of swyx's most influential conceptual contributions is the systematization of the evolution of software in three eras:

- **Software 1.0**: traditional handwritten software, where every decision is explicitly coded by the programmer in a formal programming language. The system's behavior is entirely determined by the source code.

- **Software 2.0**: coined by Andrej Karpathy, describes the paradigm where neural networks are trained on data to solve specific tasks (computer vision, specialized NLP, recommendation). The "code" is the network weights, and the "programmer" is the training process. Each model solves a single domain.

- **Software 3.0**: the current paradigm where **foundation models** (LLMs) encode decisions that in previous iterations would have been hard-coded into the software. Instead of running machine learning for a single domain, you run machine learning across **all domains**, creating fundamental models. Programming happens in natural language via prompts, and the LLM itself handles the decision-making logic of the application.

The transition to Software 3.0 has profound implications for those who build software: developers no longer have to bring data or develop ML pipelines to obtain AI functionality -- this functionality is accessible behind LLM APIs. "Vibe coding" (term popularized by Karpathy) -- the practice of writing code simply by telling an AI program what you want -- is a concrete manifestation of this paradigm.

### 5.2 The AI Engineer Landscape: the map of the field

In his talk "Software 3.0 and the AI Engineer Landscape" (presented in various iterations, including CascadiaJS 2024), swyx proposes a structured mental model to navigate the AI ecosystem. His motivation is explicitly practical: "there is a lot of noise, hype and demos in AI, but there is not much practical guidance" -- the landscape provides a framework for understanding who is doing what and where opportunities exist.

Wang identifies **seven specialization disciplines** for AI engineers:

1. **Prompt Engineering and Structured Responses** -- the creation of effective interactions with LLMs, including the use of structured outputs (JSON, function calling)
2. **AI UX** -- the design of user experiences around AI capabilities, a field still largely unexplored
3. **OSS AI - Hosting and Infrastructure** -- the deployment and management of open-source models
4. **OSS AI - Finetuning and Evaluations** -- the customization and testing of models
5. **AI Productivity Devtools** -- the construction of AI-augmented developer tools
6. **Tooling for AI** -- the creation of infrastructure for AI development
7. **AI Agents** -- the construction of autonomous AI systems

The landscape is also articulated in **levels** ranging from foundation models (OpenAI, Anthropic) to frameworks and libraries (LangChain, LlamaIndex, Haystack, Vercel AI SDK, Semantic Kernel, Guardrails, Guidance), to vector databases, OSS hosting, up to monitoring and observability. A crucial insight from swyx is that "giving equal weight to all categories is a mistake" -- there is a hierarchy of importance, not a flat categorization.

### 5.3 Prompt Engineering vs Context Engineering

Swyx was one of the first to popularize the distinction between **prompt engineering** and **context engineering**, a framework that then became mainstream in the industry in 2025.

**Prompt Engineering** focuses on "how do you formulate the question?" -- it operates within a single input-output exchange, and concerns the crafting of clear and well-structured instructions for the LLM. It is a subset of context engineering, not the other way around.

**Context Engineering** focuses on "what information must the model have access to at this moment, and why should it care?" -- it concerns the design of the **entire flow and architecture** of the model's thought process. It includes the management of memory, conversational history, available tools, system prompt, and everything the model "sees" when it receives an input.

The difference in mentality is fundamental: prompt engineering is analogous to writing a single subroutine, while context engineering is analogous to **software architecture** -- a systemic and repeatable approach. The goal of context engineering is to ensure that the model performs consistently across different sessions, different users and unpredictable conditions. Practical applications include LLM agents with memory, customer support chatbots that don't hallucinate, multi-turn flows, and production systems that need predictability.

### 5.4 Cognitive Architectures for Language Agents

One of the most influential episodes of Latent Space was the interview with **Shunyu Yao** (Princeton), creator of ReAct and co-author of CoALA, with **Harrison Chase** (LangChain) as guest host. This episode helped crystallize swyx's thinking on cognitive architectures for agents.

The **CoALA** (Cognitive Architectures for Language Agents) framework, discussed in depth in the episode, draws from the rich history of cognitive sciences and symbolic AI to describe a language agent with:

- **Modular memory components** -- working memory (which persists across LLM calls), long-term memory, and grounding interfaces
- **Structured action space** -- to interact with internal memory and external environments
- **Generalized decision-making process** -- to choose actions based on context

Working memory in CoALA functions as a central hub: the LLM input is synthesized from a subset of working memory, and the output is parsed into variables that are reinserted into working memory. This mechanism creates a continuous cycle of reasoning and action.

A particularly significant concept emerged from the discussion is that of **AI-Computer Interfaces (ACI)** -- interfaces designed specifically for agents, analogous to how Human-Computer Interfaces (HCI) are designed for humans. This distinction suggests that the optimal tools for AI agents may be fundamentally different from those designed for human operators.

## 6. Key Insights on Agents

### The IMPACT framework

In his keynote at the AI Engineer Summit 2025, swyx presented the **IMPACT framework**, a comprehensive model for understanding and building AI agents through six essential elements:

- **I - Intent**: multimodal input that encodes goals, verified through evaluations executed in specific environments. Intent is not simply a prompt -- it is the structured translation of what the user wants to achieve.

- **M - Memory**: long-term systems that create coherence and self-improvement cycles, including structured skill libraries. Memory is not just context -- it is the agent's ability to learn from its operational experience.

- **P - Planning**: multi-step operations with editable plans as current best practice. Swyx emphasizes that human guidance through editable plans represents the state of the art for multi-step reasoning.

- **A - Authority**: delegated trust mechanisms, with a crucial distinction between "trust but verify" approaches (read-heavy, suitable for low-risk contexts) and enterprise-grade governance. As swyx underlines: "if there is no trust, there is no agent" -- delegation without governance creates "stutter-step" friction, particularly problematic in enterprise contexts.

- **C - Control Flow**: LLM-driven decision-making that determines the application's behavior. The agent's degree of autonomy correlates directly with how much the LLM controls the application flow, distinguishing autonomous agents from rigid workflows.

- **T - Tools**: universal consensus on tool use, including RAG, sandbox, browser automation. Tools are the interface between the agent and the external world.

### The agent formula

Swyx proposed a synthetic definition that has become one of the most cited references in the field:

> **agent = LLM + memory + planning + tools + while loop**

This formula captures the essence of what distinguishes an agent from a simple chatbot: the **while loop** -- the continuous cycle of perception, reasoning and action that allows the agent to operate autonomously until the goal is reached.

### Critique of existing frameworks

Swyx critically observes that "people always over-emphasize the point from which they start" when defining agents. He specifically cites OpenAI's **TRIM framework**, which in his view omits crucial considerations on memory, planning and authorization that deserve specialized attention in agent engineering. This critique highlights the tendency of labs to propose frameworks that reflect their own strengths rather than the complete needs of the field.

### LLM-in-the-Loop: the architectural principle

The concept of **"LLM-in-the-Loop"** proposed by swyx defines the spectrum of agent autonomy: the more an LLM controls the application flow, the closer the system is to an autonomous agent. At the two extremes of the spectrum we find rigid workflows (where the LLM is simply a component called at predefined points) and fully autonomous agents (where the LLM dynamically decides every subsequent step). This principle helps designers position their systems along the continuum and make informed architectural choices.

### smol developer: the practical agent

In 2023, Wang launched **smol developer**, described as "the first library to embed a developer agent in your app". The project embodies swyx's pragmatic philosophy: a bundle of prompts and a resilient workflow built on Modal, which represents one of the first attempts to build a generalist software agent.

The smol developer approach is distinctive for its emphasis on **human-in-the-loop**: the AI is used only as long as it adds value -- when it becomes an obstacle, the user can take control of the codebase "without stories and without hurt feelings". This philosophy reflects a mature understanding of the current limits of agents and a preference for practical incrementalism over the promise of complete autonomy.

### 2025 as the "Year of Agents" (with constructive skepticism)

Swyx commented with his typical constructive skepticism: "Everyone says that 2025 is the year of agents, just like they said last year". However, he recognizes that in 2025 agents have actually been the biggest development story, and "agentic" was the word of the year (again). The **Model Context Protocol (MCP)** has become ubiquitous -- running an MCP server has become almost as common as running a web server -- representing fundamental infrastructure for agent interoperability.

Swyx's connection with **Cognition** (the creators of Devin, the first autonomous coding agent) represents a further evolution of his path: in 2025 he announced that he had joined Cognition in a capacity yet to be defined, while Latent Space and AI Engineer remain independent. This positioning places him directly at the heart of coding agent development.
