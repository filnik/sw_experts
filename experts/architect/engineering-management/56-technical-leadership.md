# Technical Leadership

## Profile

### What It Is
Technical Leadership is the practice of guiding technical direction, mentoring engineers, making architectural decisions, and enabling teams to deliver high-quality software. It bridges the gap between management and engineering, combining deep technical expertise with communication, influence, and organizational skills.

### Origin and Evolution
- Engineering management as distinct discipline (1990s-2000s)
- Staff/Principal engineer roles formalized (2010s)
- Will Larson's *Staff Engineer* and *An Elegant Puzzle* defined modern tech leadership
- IC (Individual Contributor) leadership track parallel to management track
- Current: Tech Lead, Staff Engineer, Principal Engineer, Distinguished Engineer ladder

## Core Philosophy

### The 5 Fundamental Principles
1. **Multiply through others** — A technical leader's impact is measured by the team's output, not personal code contributions.
2. **Make technical decisions explicit** — Document decisions (ADRs), communicate rationale, build consensus rather than dictating.
3. **Create psychological safety** — Teams that feel safe to disagree, ask questions, and admit mistakes produce better work.
4. **Balance technical depth with breadth** — Stay technically sharp while developing organizational awareness and communication skills.
5. **Manage technical direction, not people** — Tech leads guide what to build and how; managers guide who does what and career growth.

### When to Use vs. When to Avoid

**Tech leadership is essential when:**
- Teams need technical direction and architectural decisions
- Junior engineers need mentoring and growth opportunities
- Cross-team technical alignment is required
- Technical debt and standards need championing

**Delegate when:**
- Team is experienced and self-directing
- Decisions are within team expertise
- Micromanagement would slow the team

## Frameworks and Methodologies

### 1. Technical Decision-Making — step-by-step
1. Gather context: requirements, constraints, team capabilities
2. Identify options with tradeoffs (at least 2-3 alternatives)
3. Consult stakeholders: team, adjacent teams, product
4. Make the decision (or facilitate the team making it)
5. Document in an ADR with rationale
6. Communicate broadly: why this decision, what it means

### 2. Mentoring Engineers — step-by-step
1. Understand the engineer's goals and current level
2. Assign stretch tasks that build specific skills
3. Pair on complex problems (knowledge transfer through practice)
4. Provide specific, actionable feedback on code and design
5. Advocate for their growth in promotion/review discussions
6. Gradually increase their autonomy as skills grow

## How to Apply

### Decision Checklist
- [ ] Am I multiplying the team's effectiveness, not just writing code myself?
- [ ] Are technical decisions documented and communicated?
- [ ] Is the team comfortable raising concerns and disagreeing?
- [ ] Am I mentoring team members toward independence?
- [ ] Am I staying technically relevant while leading?

### Common Mistakes
1. **Hero coder** — Writing all the complex code yourself instead of teaching others
2. **Ivory tower architect** — Making decisions without understanding implementation reality
3. **No delegation** — Keeping all interesting work; team doesn't grow
4. **Avoiding conflict** — Not addressing technical disagreements, letting them fester
5. **All tech, no people** — Ignoring team dynamics, burnout, and career development
